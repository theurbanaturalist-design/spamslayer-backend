ALTERNATIVES IF YOU CANNOT REACH THIS DEFENDANT
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Your preliminary collectability score is LOW. Read this section
carefully BEFORE spending a filing fee. A judgment you cannot
collect is worse than no judgment at all — it costs money, time,
and hope, and buys you nothing.

1. SUE UP THE CHAIN (seller liability)
   ───────────────────────────────────
   The TCPA makes the SELLER — the company on whose behalf the calls
   were placed — liable for calls made by their agents, even offshore
   agents. This was settled by the FCC's 2013 DISH Network ruling
   (In re Joint Petition, 28 FCC Rcd 6574) and has been affirmed by
   multiple circuits.

   WHAT TO DO: listen to the recordings and identify the PRODUCT or
   the ISSUER. Auto-warranty calls ultimately pitch a named company's
   contract (CarShield, Endurance, Omega Auto Care, etc.). Solar calls
   pitch a named installer. Medicare calls pitch a named insurer.
   That named company IS the defendant — not the offshore call center,
   not the spoofed number.

   Look for phrases like 'on behalf of ___', 'we work with ___', or
   'your policy would be with ___'. Those fill-in-the-blanks name
   the real defendant.

2. FILE ADMINISTRATIVE COMPLAINTS IN PARALLEL
   ──────────────────────────────────────────
   These do not pay you directly, but they (a) create a public paper
   trail that class-action firms actively mine, (b) give carriers the
   leverage to block the number at network level under the TRACED Act
   and STIR/SHAKEN rules, and (c) occasionally trigger an enforcement
   action that results in consumer-restitution funds.

       1. FCC TCPA Complaint: https://consumercomplaints.fcc.gov/
       2. FTC Do Not Call: https://www.donotcall.gov/report.html
       3. State Attorney General consumer-protection complaint
          (search '[your state] attorney general consumer complaint')
       4. Better Business Bureau: https://www.bbb.org/file-a-complaint
       5. CFPB (for debt-relief / financial pitches):
          https://www.consumerfinance.gov/complaint/

   File all five for each offender. It takes about 20 minutes total.
   Include the specific dates/times of calls and attach your evidence
   if the form allows uploads.

3. REQUEST AN ORIGINATING-CARRIER TRACE
   ────────────────────────────────────
   Under the TRACED Act and FCC rules (47 CFR § 64.1200(k)), carriers
   must cooperate to trace the originating carrier of spoofed calls.
   Your own carrier's fraud/abuse team can initiate this trace on your
   behalf. The originating carrier then has records of the customer
   who bought the number — that customer is usually a US-based VoIP
   reseller whose agreements with their downstream customer (the
   telemarketer) are discoverable.

   WHAT TO DO: call your carrier, ask for the fraud department,
   explicitly request a 'STIR/SHAKEN traceback' for calls from
   +1 (555) 555-0199 on the specific dates in your evidence log. Some
   carriers route this through the Industry Traceback Group (ITG)
   at https://tracebacks.org — you can also file directly with the ITG.

7. LEAD-GENERATOR / DATA-BROKER LIABILITY
   ──────────────────────────────────────
   Under TCPA, a lead-generator or data broker who sold your number
   to a telemarketer can be liable under § 227(c) if you did not
   provide lawful prior express written consent. Lead generators are
   almost always US-based and almost always have assets.

   WHAT TO DO: during any call where the caller claims consent, ask
   'WHICH website / form / offer did I fill out?' Write down the
   answer. Then research whether that site is a known lead-generator
   (CPA-networks like MediaAlpha, QuoteWizard, SmartFinancial, etc.)
   and whether their consent flow would have been TCPA-compliant for
   calls to DNC-registered numbers. Often it is not.
