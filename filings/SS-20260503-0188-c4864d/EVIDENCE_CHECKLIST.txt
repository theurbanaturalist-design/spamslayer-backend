  [   ]  1. File ITG traceback request

        WHY:
          ITG (Industry Traceback Group, FCC-designated U.S. Traceback
          Consortium) traces the call back through the carrier chain to its
          origin carrier. Their report is a quasi-governmental third-party
          record that's been admitted in TCPA cases. File within ~5 days of
          the most recent call for best traceback success — older calls have a
          much lower trace-completion rate.

        URL: https://tracebacks.org/traceback-requests/

        COPY-PASTE TEMPLATE:
        ──────────────────────
          Originating call number: +15555550188
          Called number (recipient): [YOUR PHONE]
          Number of calls: 2
          Date range: 2026-05-03 to 2026-05-03
          All call timestamps: 2026-05-03 14:04, 2026-05-03 14:04
          Recipient's status on DNC Registry: registered
          Caller's stated company: National Auto Protection
          Caller's stated name: Brad
          Stated purpose: warranty
          
          These calls were answered by an automated TCPA compliance recording on a residential telephone number registered with the National Do Not Call Registry. Each call was recorded under one-party consent. Recording URLs and transcripts are available on request.
        ──────────────────────

        WHAT TO EXPECT:
          WHAT YOU'LL SEE: a single-page web form titled 'Traceback Request'. Fields: your name, your email, the originating phone number, the called number, the date(s) and time(s), and a description box.
          
          WHAT TO DO: paste the template above into the description field — it's pre-formatted with everything ITG asks for. Use your real name and email. Submit.
          
          HOW LONG: ~3 min to fill out, no account needed.
          
          WHAT HAPPENS NEXT: ITG emails an acknowledgment within 24 hours with a tracking number. Save that email. They'll work the traceback through participating carriers (~1-3 weeks). If successful, you'll get a follow-up email naming the originating carrier. If unsuccessful (call too old, or unparticipating carrier in the chain), they'll tell you so — that itself is useful evidence.
          
          WHAT TO WATCH FOR: don't include profanity or threats in the description — ITG will reject the request. Also don't claim damages amounts here; that's for the petition, not the traceback request.

        WHY:
          Parallel enforcement pressure. The FCC complaint generates a docket
          number that goes into the Robocall Mitigation Database — both a
          third-party record AND a signal to the defendant's carrier that the
          calls are flagged. Doesn't compete with your civil suit; it
          complements it.

        URL: https://consumercomplaints.fcc.gov/hc/en-us/requests/new

        COPY-PASTE TEMPLATE:
        ──────────────────────
          Use these exact field values in the FCC form:
          
            Type of complaint:        Unwanted calls (telemarketing/robocalls)
            Phone number that was called: [YOUR PHONE]
            Caller phone number:      +15555550188
            Caller name (if known):   National Auto Protection
            Date(s) of call(s):       2026-05-03 14:04, 2026-05-03 14:04
            On DNC Registry:          Yes
            Description: I received 2 unsolicited telemarketing calls from +15555550188 between 2026-05-03 and 2026-05-03. My number is registered on the National Do Not Call Registry. Each call was recorded under one-party consent (La. R.S. 15:1303); audio and transcripts are available on request. The calls violated 47 U.S.C. § 227(c) and 47 C.F.R. § 64.1200.
        ──────────────────────

        WHAT TO EXPECT:
          WHAT YOU'LL SEE: the FCC's Zendesk-styled complaint portal. You'll create a free account (or log in via Google) before submitting. The form is split into 'About you', 'About the call', and 'Description'.
          
          WHAT TO DO: paste the field values above into the matching form fields. The Description field accepts ~2000 characters — you have room to mention recordings exist.
          
          HOW LONG: ~5 min including account creation.
          
          WHAT HAPPENS NEXT: FCC sends a confirmation email with a complaint number (format: 25NNNNNN). Save that email — the complaint number is your third-party docket reference. The complaint goes into the FCC's public Robocall Mitigation Database; some defendants' carriers (Twilio, Bandwidth, etc.) actively monitor that DB and may suspend the offender's outbound calling.
          
          WHAT NOT TO DO: don't claim a specific damages amount in the FCC form. The FCC doesn't award damages; that's for your civil suit. Stating amounts here can box you in later.

  [   ]  7. Send demand letter via certified mail (return receipt requested)

        WHY:
          The demand letter starts the 30-day clock and converts post-demand
          calls into willful/treble damages ($1,500 per call instead of $500).
          Even if the defendant ignores it, you've created a legal record that
          puts them on notice — that record itself becomes Exhibit B in your
          petition.

        COPY-PASTE TEMPLATE:
        ──────────────────────
          The demand letter is auto-generated. Pull it via:
          
            curl -X POST http://localhost:3003/api/cases/demand-letter \
              -H 'Content-Type: application/json' \
              -d '{"number":"+15555550188","userName":"[YOUR NAME]","userAddress":"[YOUR ADDRESS]","userPhone":"[YOUR PHONE]","dncSince":"YYYY-MM-DD"}'
          
          Or pull it from the dashboard if you've added a UI button.
        ──────────────────────

        WHAT TO EXPECT:
          WHAT YOU'LL DO:
            1. Generate the demand letter (the API call above produces a fully-formatted certified-mail-ready letter).
            2. Print it. Sign + date the bottom.
            3. Walk to USPS. Buy a Certified Mail label (~$4.85) AND a Return Receipt — the GREEN CARD (~$3.65). Total ~$8.50.
            4. Mail to the defendant's address. If you don't have an address, mail to their registered agent (use the OpenCorporates or state SoS lookup; for spoofed-caller-ID cases this might not be possible).
            5. When the green card comes back ~2 weeks later, scan it. That's Exhibit B.
          
          WHAT YOU'RE WAITING FOR: 30 days from delivery date (per the green card). Calls that arrive AFTER that date are 'willful' under § 227(c)(5), trebling damages. Track those calls — they're worth $1500 each instead of $500.
          
          WHAT NOT TO DO: don't email the demand letter — the legal weight of certified mail is its delivery proof, which only certified-mail green cards provide. Email is too easy to deny receipt. Don't threaten criminal prosecution in the demand letter — TCPA is purely civil.
